\begin{abstract}
Existing digital wellness applications largely function as static trackers, offering generalized feedback without accommodating individual behavioral rhythms or physiological context such as menstrual cycle dynamics. This paper presents LunaAura, a full-stack explainable machine learning framework for hormonal and emotional health analysis. The system integrates a deterministic composite risk scoring formula, a calibrated HistGradientBoosting classifier, quantile-based distributional regression for uncertainty-aware PHQ-9 estimation, and SHAP-driven per-instance explanations into a unified longitudinal monitoring platform. Six daily behavioral signals---sleep duration, perceived stress, physical activity, anxiety, hydration, and menstrual cycle day---are synthesized into personalized wellness estimates and risk indicators through a transparent weighted composite formula. For female users, additive menstrual phase modifiers grounded in chronobiological literature adjust baseline risk; for male and non-binary users, factor weights are re-normalized to preserve analytical depth. Evaluation on a controlled synthetic cohort of 105 users across 470 daily log entries demonstrates the formula's sensitivity to clinically meaningful input variations. The platform is positioned as a decision-support research prototype with honest disclosure of limitations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Mental health conditions affect roughly one in eight people globally, yet the gap between those who need support and those who receive it remains wide~\cite{who2022mental}. University students face compounding pressures---academic workloads, financial stress, and disrupted sleep routines---that frequently go unmonitored until clinically significant~\cite{adler2022burnout}. Standardized instruments like the PHQ-9~\cite{kroenke2001phq9} provide validated snapshots, but they were designed for periodic clinical encounters rather than continuous monitoring.

Smartphones have opened new avenues for longitudinal behavioral tracking. Digital phenotyping research has shown that passively collected signals correlate with depressive symptom severity~\cite{saeb2015mobile, torous2019digital}. Yet most consumer wellness apps reduce this potential to simplistic trackers offering no personalization or analytical depth.

Three gaps motivate this work. First, most tools apply identical thresholds regardless of individual baselines. Second, few systems account for menstrual cycle dynamics despite evidence that hormonal fluctuations modulate sleep, stress reactivity, and mood~\cite{baker2007sleep, kiesner2012menstrual}. Third, interpretability remains an afterthought---users receive scores without understanding which factors drive them.

LunaAura addresses these gaps through a personalized, explainable, cycle-aware behavioral analytics framework. The principal contributions are: (1)~a transparent six-factor composite risk formula with cycle-phase modifiers; (2)~a gender-adaptive scoring architecture; (3)~integration of calibrated classification, quantile regression, and SHAP explainability; and (4)~a complete open-source implementation with interactive longitudinal dashboard.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{ML for Mental Health Screening}
Kroenke et al.\ established the PHQ-9 as a reliable depression measure~\cite{kroenke2001phq9}. Arefin and de Roux~\cite{arefin2020machine} demonstrated ensemble classifiers for depressive language detection, though on cross-sectional data rather than longitudinal streams.

\subsection{Digital Phenotyping}
Saeb et al.~\cite{saeb2015mobile} showed phone sensor correlates of PHQ-9 scores. The MONARCA system~\cite{bardram2020designing} extended passive sensing to bipolar disorder. Torous et al.~\cite{torous2019digital, torous2021digital} noted data quality and interpretability as barriers to clinical adoption.

\subsection{Explainable AI}
SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017shap} provides theoretically grounded feature attribution. In health contexts, transparent feedback encourages sustained platform engagement~\cite{rachuri2010emotionsense}.

\subsection{Cycle-Aware Health Systems}
Baker and Driver~\cite{baker2007sleep} documented sleep architecture differences across menstrual phases. Kiesner~\cite{kiesner2012menstrual} found paradoxically opposite mood effects across individuals, underscoring the need for personalized baselines. Few computational systems incorporate this evidence.

\subsection{Calibration and Uncertainty}
Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999calibration} provides post-hoc probability calibration, while quantile regression~\cite{meinshausen2006quantile, koenker2001quantile} offers distributional uncertainty estimates---particularly relevant where honest bounds outweigh point predictions.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{System Architecture}

LunaAura follows a three-tier architecture: a static frontend (HTML/JavaScript/Chart.js), a Flask REST API backend (port 5001), and a SQLite persistence layer. The backend orchestrates four subsystems: (i)~a hybrid prediction engine, (ii)~a SHAP TreeExplainer, (iii)~a rules-based recommendation generator, and (iv)~a chart simulation module. Seven API endpoints serve the application: \texttt{/health}, \texttt{/signup}, \texttt{/login}, \texttt{/profile}, \texttt{/predict}, \texttt{/analytics}, and \texttt{/insights}.

\subsection{Database and Cohort}

The SQLite database contains a \texttt{users} table (12 columns: demographics, cycle length, sleep target) and a \texttt{user\_history} table (12 columns: daily behavioral logs linked via foreign key). A controlled synthetic cohort (seed~=~42) comprises 105 users across three risk strata (30 high, 40 moderate, 30 low), with gender split 86F/14M/5O, ages 18--55, and 470 daily log entries. One user (Eshita) has 85 sequential entries with cycle-phase-driven variation.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

The ML pipeline operates on a master dataset ($n=9{,}548$, 28 columns) merging sleep-lifestyle data with PHQ-9 responses via K-NN linking. Ten features are retained: Age, Sleep Duration, Physical Activity Level, Stress Level, Base Cycle Length, Cycle Day, Hormone Proxy (sinusoidal cycle encoding), and three rolling-window features---Sleep Rolling Mean (3-day), Stress Rolling Mean (3-day), and Stress Volatility (3-day~$\sigma$).

\subsection{Composite Risk Formula}

The primary scoring mechanism is a deterministic weighted composite, chosen for transparency and reproducibility. The composite risk $R$ is:

\begin{equation}
R = \sum_{i=1}^{5} f_i(x_i) + \tau + \phi(d, g)
\label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}

Each factor contribution is defined as:

\begin{align}
f_1 &= \frac{x_{\text{stress}}}{10} \times 35 \label{eq:f1}\\
f_2 &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{8 - x_{\text{sleep}}}{8}\right) \times 25 \label{eq:f2}\\
f_3 &= \frac{x_{\text{anxiety}}}{10} \times 20 \label{eq:f3}\\
f_4 &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{60 - x_{\text{act}}}{60}\right) \times 10 \label{eq:f4}\\
f_5 &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{2.5 - x_{\text{water}}}{2.5}\right) \times 5 \label{eq:f5}
\end{align}

The trend baseline $\tau = 5$ is a fixed constant. The cycle-phase modifier for female users ($g = \text{Female}$) is:

\begin{equation}
\phi(d, g) = \begin{cases}
+5 & d \in [1, 5] \;\text{(Menstrual)}\\
-5 & d \in [6, 13] \;\text{(Follicular)}\\
-8 & d \in [14, 16] \;\text{(Ovulatory)}\\
+8 & d \in [17, 28] \;\text{(Luteal)}\\
0  & g \neq \text{Female}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:phase}
\end{equation}

Risk is clamped to $[0, 100]$ and classified: Low ($R < 35$), Moderate ($35 \leq R < 65$), High ($R \geq 65$).

\subsection{Wellness Score}

A complementary wellness score aggregates normalized dimensions $\hat{x}_j \in [0, 100]$:

\begin{equation}
W = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \alpha_j \cdot \hat{x}_j, \quad W \in [0, 100]
\label{eq:wellness}
\end{equation}

For female users ($k{=}8$): Sleep 25\%, Stress 20\%, Activity 15\%, Anxiety 15\%, Cycle 10\%, Water 5\%, Age 5\%, Stability 5\%. For male/other ($k{=}6$): Sleep 30\%, Stress 25\%, Activity 20\%, Anxiety 15\%, Water 5\%, Age 5\%. Normalization functions: $\hat{x}_{\text{sleep}} = 100 - |x_{\text{sleep}} - 8| \times 15$, \; $\hat{x}_{\text{stress}} = 100 - (x_{\text{stress}} - 1) \times 11.1$, \; $\hat{x}_{\text{act}} = \min(100, \; x_{\text{act}} / 60 \times 100)$.

\subsection{Machine Learning Pipeline}

\textbf{Calibrated Classifier.} A \texttt{HistGradientBoostingClassifier} ($\text{max\_iter}{=}100$, $\text{max\_leaf\_nodes}{=}31$, $\eta{=}0.05$) predicts PHQ-9-derived referral flags ($n_0{=}5{,}054$, $n_1{=}4{,}494$). Post-hoc sigmoid calibration~\cite{platt1999calibration} via 5-fold stratified CV produces calibrated probabilities:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{cal}}(y{=}1 \mid x) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(A \cdot s(x) + B)}
\label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Quantile Regressors.} Three \texttt{HistGradientBoostingRegressor} models ($\text{max\_iter}{=}150$, $q \in \{0.10, 0.50, 0.90\}$) target PHQ-9 score quantiles using pinball loss~\cite{meinshausen2006quantile}:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_{q} = \arg\min_{y} \sum_{i} \rho_q(y_i - y), \; \rho_q(u) = u(q - \mathbf{1}_{u<0})
\label{eq:quantile}
\end{equation}

\textbf{SHAP Explainer.} A \texttt{RandomForestClassifier} ($n{=}100$, depth${=}5$) serves as SHAP proxy. TreeExplainer~\cite{lundberg2017shap} computes Shapley values:

\begin{equation}
\phi_j = \sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!(|F|{-}|S|{-}1)!}{|F|!} [f(S {\cup} \{j\}) - f(S)]
\label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}

The top three contributing factors are surfaced as natural-language explanations.

\section{Results and Discussion}

\subsection{Model Evaluation}

The calibrated classifier achieves AUROC~$\approx$~0.567 on train-test split with Brier score~0.245. This modest performance is expected: the training data bridges heterogeneous datasets using generic variables, creating inherent noise. Rather than inflating metrics, LunaAura relies on the deterministic formula for primary scoring, using ML for supplementary estimation and explainability.

Fig.~\ref{fig:roc} presents the ROC curve and Fig.~\ref{fig:feat} shows feature importance from the SHAP proxy, confirming that Age, Stress Level, and Cycle Day are the strongest predictive signals.

\subsection{Hybrid Architecture Rationale}

The deterministic formula provides user-facing scores with full transparency. The calibrated classifier contributes probability estimates informed by the broader PHQ-9 distribution. Quantile regressors supply uncertainty intervals no deterministic formula can provide. The SHAP explainer generates per-instance attributions as human-readable explanations. Each component addresses a distinct facet of the analytical pipeline.

\subsection{Limitations}

The cohort is synthetically generated; while distributions span realistic ranges, organic behavioral correlations are absent. The composite formula uses expert-derived weights not optimized on clinical data. The classifier's modest AUROC reflects heterogeneous data bridging---we consider honest reporting preferable to inflation. The system produces Behavioral Wellness Estimates, not clinical diagnoses.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

LunaAura demonstrates that a personalized, explainable, cycle-aware wellness framework is feasible using established ML techniques combined with transparent deterministic scoring. The system's strength lies in its integrative architecture combining composite formulas, calibrated ML, quantile uncertainty, SHAP explainability, and longitudinal visualization.

Future work includes: (i)~validation on clinically collected longitudinal data, (ii)~wearable sensor integration for passive physiological signals~\cite{torous2021digital}, (iii)~federated learning for privacy-preserving distributed model improvement~\cite{wu2021federated}, (iv)~Bayesian weight optimization, and (v)~mobile-native deployment with individual cycle irregularity modeling.

The codebase, models, and evaluation assets are available in the project repository for independent reproduction.
