\begin{abstract}
Contemporary wellness tracking tools tend to reduce complex behavioral health into isolated metrics---step counts, sleep logs, or single-question mood ratings---offering little analytical depth and no adaptation to individual physiology. LunaAura reimagines this paradigm by coupling a deterministic, auditable risk scoring formula with gradient-boosted probabilistic classification, three-quantile distributional regression on PHQ-9 outcomes, and instance-level SHAP explanations, all served through an interactive longitudinal dashboard. Users report six behavioral dimensions daily: sleep hours, subjective stress, exercise minutes, anxiety, water consumption, and menstrual cycle position. A weighted composite function translates these inputs into a risk indicator and a complementary wellness estimate, with additive phase-specific adjustments for users tracking hormonal cycles and a re-normalized weight set for those who are not. The engineering stack---Flask API, SQLite storage, Chart.js visualization---ensures that every analytical output is traceable from raw input to rendered chart. Testing against a reproducible 105-user synthetic cohort with 470 daily records, supplemented by a 40-day single-subject longitudinal study, validates the scoring function's graded response to behavioral variation. This work is presented as a research-stage decision-support tool; no claim of clinical diagnostic capability is made.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Nearly 970 million people live with a mental health condition, and the treatment gap in low- and middle-income countries exceeds 75\% \cite{who2022mental}. Within student populations, the convergence of academic pressure, erratic sleep schedules, financial insecurity, and social fragmentation creates a particularly fertile ground for subclinical distress to take root unnoticed \cite{adler2022burnout}. Instruments such as the PHQ-9 \cite{kroenke2001phq9} capture symptom severity with well-characterized psychometric properties, but their design presupposes a clinical encounter---a scheduled visit where a practitioner administers the questionnaire and interprets the result. What remains largely unaddressed is the space between formal assessments: the weeks of gradually worsening sleep, creeping anxiety, and declining physical activity that precede a diagnosable episode.

Smartphones, in principle, could fill that gap. The digital phenotyping movement has shown that patterns extracted from device sensors---location entropy, call frequency, screen-on duration---track meaningful variance in PHQ-9 scores \cite{saeb2015mobile, torous2019digital}. In practice, though, commercial wellness applications have proven disappointing. They collect data eagerly but return little of substance: a congratulatory badge for hitting ten thousand steps, a suggestion to ``try deep breathing,'' or a color-coded mood log with no analytical backbone. Users lose interest quickly because the system offers nothing they could not infer on their own.

We identify three specific design failures that perpetuate this problem. The first is dimensional flatness. Evaluating sleep in isolation from stress, exercise, or hydration misses the combinatorial nature of behavioral risk---six hours of sleep means something very different depending on what else is happening in a person's day. The second is biological blindness. The menstrual cycle reshapes sleep quality, cortisol reactivity, and affective regulation across a roughly four-week rhythm \cite{baker2007sleep, mcewen2008central}, yet almost no scoring model acknowledges this. A female user in her luteal phase, experiencing hormonally mediated sleep disruption and heightened stress sensitivity \cite{kiesner2012menstrual}, receives the same assessment as one in her follicular phase, when hormonal conditions are broadly favorable. The third failure is opacity. When a system does compute a score, it rarely explains which inputs mattered or how the user might intervene.

LunaAura was designed around these three observations. It is a research prototype---not a clinical instrument---that integrates transparent composite scoring, calibrated machine learning, distributional uncertainty quantification, and per-instance explainability into a single full-stack application. Its contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item A six-factor deterministic risk function with published, auditable weights and menstrual-cycle modifiers derived from the chronobiology literature.
\item Gender-adaptive weight normalization that maintains analytical parity for male and non-binary users by redistributing freed capacity rather than discarding features.
\item A hybrid ML pipeline pairing a sigmoid-calibrated HistGradientBoosting classifier with quantile regressors and a Random Forest SHAP proxy for instance-level explanations.
\item An end-to-end open-source implementation: data seeding, model training, Flask REST API, SQLite persistence, and a Chart.js interactive dashboard.
\end{itemize}

\section{Literature Review}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Computational Screening for Mental Health}
The PHQ-9 questionnaire, validated over two decades ago by Kroenke and colleagues \cite{kroenke2001phq9}, remains the most widely adopted self-report measure for depressive symptom severity. Its nine items map to DSM-IV criteria and produce a 0--27 score stratified into severity bands (Minimal, Mild, Moderate, Moderately Severe, Severe). Automated scoring from behavioral proxies has since become an active research direction: Arefin and de Roux \cite{arefin2020machine} applied ensemble classifiers to social media text, distinguishing depressive from non-depressive language with moderate accuracy but no longitudinal tracking. Tree-based methods, particularly Random Forests \cite{breiman2001random}, continue to dominate tabular health prediction tasks because they tolerate missing data gracefully and expose feature importance rankings without additional post-hoc analysis.

\subsection{Digital Phenotyping}
Saeb and colleagues \cite{saeb2015mobile} offered early evidence that GPS traces and phone usage statistics predict PHQ-9 variation at the individual level. The MONARCA project \cite{bardram2020designing} brought similar ideas to bipolar disorder, combining accelerometer data with daily self-reports in a clinician-facing dashboard. Torous et al.\ have written extensively on the promises and pitfalls of this paradigm \cite{torous2019digital, torous2021digital}, arguing that without rigorous attention to data provenance, consent, and interpretability, digital phenotyping risks producing noise dressed up as insight.

\subsection{Interpretable Machine Learning}
Lundberg and Lee's SHAP framework \cite{lundberg2017shap} placed feature attribution on firm game-theoretic ground by computing exact Shapley values for each predictor. In clinical decision support, explainability is not optional: a model that flags a patient without saying why is unlikely to earn a clinician's trust or a regulator's approval. Work on EmotionSense \cite{rachuri2010emotionsense} found that users interacted more frequently and for longer durations with wellness tools that offered contextual, interpretable feedback compared to those that displayed raw numbers alone.

\subsection{Menstrual Cycle and Behavioral Regulation}
Hormonal oscillations across the menstrual cycle influence multiple domains relevant to wellness tracking. Baker and Driver \cite{baker2007sleep} measured reduced slow-wave sleep amplitude during the luteal phase, a period when progesterone peaks. Kiesner \cite{kiesner2012menstrual} complicated the picture by showing that the direction of luteal-phase mood change varies substantially across individuals---some women experience improvement, others deterioration---making population-level averages a poor proxy for individual experience. McEwen's allostatic load model \cite{mcewen2008central} provides a mechanistic account of how chronic stress exposure interacts with hormonal cycles: cortisol dysregulation widens the vulnerability window that cyclical progesterone fluctuations create. Despite this convergent evidence, few digital health platforms incorporate cycle-phase awareness into their analytical engines.

\subsection{Calibration and Distributional Prediction}
Platt's sigmoid recalibration method \cite{platt1999calibration} transforms the raw output of a binary classifier into a probability that better reflects true class prevalence---a critical step when predicted probabilities inform downstream decisions. Quantile regression, formalized by Koenker and Hallock \cite{koenker2001quantile} and extended to tree-based models by Meinshausen \cite{meinshausen2006quantile}, replaces point prediction with a distributional envelope: instead of a single estimate, the model returns a credible interval (e.g., 10th--90th percentile) that honestly represents its uncertainty. In behavioral health, where individual variability is large and consequences of overconfidence are real, this transparency is especially important.

\section{Methodology}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Architecture Overview}
The platform separates concerns into three layers. A static frontend built with HTML, vanilla JavaScript, and Chart.js handles user interaction and visualization. A Python Flask server, listening on port 5001, exposes seven RESTful endpoints: \texttt{/health} for liveness checks, \texttt{/signup} and \texttt{/login} for credential management, \texttt{/profile} for demographic editing, \texttt{/predict} for inference with simultaneous database logging, \texttt{/analytics} for population-level summaries, and \texttt{/insights} for model metadata. Beneath these routes sit four subsystems: a hybrid scoring engine (deterministic formula plus ML), a SHAP-based TreeExplainer, a threshold-driven recommendation generator, and a chart simulation module that projects visualization-ready arrays from current-day inputs.

All state persists in a SQLite database (\texttt{lunaaura.db}) containing two relational tables. The \texttt{users} table holds 12 fields---username, hashed password, age, gender, anthropometrics, cycle length, sleep target, cohort tag, source label, and timestamps. The \texttt{user\_history} table records 12 fields per daily log entry---sleep hours, ordinal stress (1--10), mood score, cycle day, phase label, exercise minutes, computed wellness, predicted risk, ordinal anxiety (1--10), and water intake (litres)---linked to \texttt{users} via foreign key.

\subsection{Data Generation}
Because this prototype did not involve human subjects, a synthetic cohort was generated deterministically (random seed 42) to support reproducible evaluation. The cohort contains 105 user profiles stratified into three behavioral clusters: 30 high-risk (sleep 3--5.5\,h, stress 7--10, activity 0--20\,min), 40 moderate (sleep 5.5--7\,h, stress 4--7, activity 20--60\,min), and 30 low-risk (sleep 7--9\,h, stress 1--4, activity 60--120\,min). Gender breakdown is 86 female, 14 male, 5 other; ages span 18--55 (mean 36.5). In total, 470 daily log records populate the history table. A dedicated longitudinal profile (Eshita, 85 entries over 40+ days) was seeded with cycle-phase-modulated behavioral drift---sleep shortens and stress rises during luteal and menstrual windows---to test temporal sensitivity.

\subsection{Feature Preparation}
Model training draws on a separate master dataset ($n = 9{,}548$ rows, 28 columns) assembled by linking a sleep-lifestyle survey with PHQ-9 screening data through K-nearest-neighbor matching on age and gender, then expanding each matched pair into a 14-day simulated tracking window. Ten numeric predictors survive selection: Age, Sleep Duration, Physical Activity Level, Stress Level, Base Cycle Length, Cycle Day, Hormone Proxy (sinusoidal cycle encoding), and three rolling aggregates---Sleep Rolling Mean, Stress Rolling Mean, and Stress Volatility (all computed over 3-day windows). The rolling features inject temporal context that a single-day snapshot cannot provide.

\subsection{Deterministic Risk Scoring}
The user-facing risk indicator is computed by a fully transparent weighted sum, chosen deliberately so that every output can be traced back to its constituent inputs without model introspection. The composite risk score $R$ is:

\begin{equation}
R = \sum_{i=1}^{5} f_i(x_i) \;+\; \tau \;+\; \phi(d, g)
\label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}

Individual factor terms are defined as follows. Stress contributes proportionally to its ordinal value, sleep and activity contribute through deficit functions that penalize shortfalls from healthy baselines, and anxiety and hydration contribute through analogous scaled terms:

\begin{align}
f_1 &= \frac{x_{\text{stress}}}{10} \times 35 \label{eq:f1}\\[3pt]
f_2 &= \max\!\Bigl(0,\;\frac{8 - x_{\text{sleep}}}{8}\Bigr) \times 25 \label{eq:f2}\\[3pt]
f_3 &= \frac{x_{\text{anxiety}}}{10} \times 20 \label{eq:f3}\\[3pt]
f_4 &= \max\!\Bigl(0,\;\frac{60 - x_{\text{act}}}{60}\Bigr) \times 10 \label{eq:f4}\\[3pt]
f_5 &= \max\!\Bigl(0,\;\frac{2.5 - x_{\text{water}}}{2.5}\Bigr) \times 5 \label{eq:f5}
\end{align}

A fixed trend penalty $\tau = 5$ accounts for unmeasured background drift. The hormonal phase modifier $\phi$ applies exclusively to female users:

\begin{equation}
\phi(d, g) = \begin{cases}
+5 & d \in [1,5] \;\text{(Menstrual)}\\
-5 & d \in [6,13] \;\text{(Follicular)}\\
-8 & d \in [14,16] \;\text{(Ovulatory)}\\
+8 & d \in [17,28] \;\text{(Luteal)}\\
0  & g \neq \text{Female}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:phase}
\end{equation}

The score is bounded to $[0,100]$, with severity tiers at Low ($R < 35$), Moderate ($35 \leq R < 65$), and High ($R \geq 65$).

\subsection{Wellness Estimation}

A parallel wellness index $W$ aggregates per-factor normalized scores $\hat{x}_j \in [0,100]$ under a gender-dependent weight vector $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$:

\begin{equation}
W = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \alpha_j \cdot \hat{x}_j, \quad W \in [0,100]
\label{eq:wellness}
\end{equation}

When $g = \text{Female}$, eight factors contribute ($k=8$): Sleep 25\%, Stress 20\%, Activity 15\%, Anxiety 15\%, Cycle Context 10\%, Hydration 5\%, Age 5\%, Stability 5\%. For male and non-binary profiles ($k=6$), cycle context is dropped and the released weight shifts to sleep (30\%), stress (25\%), activity (20\%), anxiety (15\%), hydration (5\%), age (5\%). Representative normalization functions: $\hat{x}_{\text{sleep}} = 100 - |x_{\text{sleep}} - 8| \times 15$; $\hat{x}_{\text{stress}} = 100 - (x_{\text{stress}} - 1) \times 11.1$; $\hat{x}_{\text{act}} = \min(100,\; x_{\text{act}}/60 \times 100)$.

\subsection{Learned Models}

Three supervised models complement the formula-based scores:

\textbf{Calibrated Classifier.} A histogram-based gradient boosting machine (\texttt{HistGradientBoostingClassifier}, 100 boosting rounds, 31 leaf nodes, $\eta = 0.05$) predicts a binary referral flag derived from PHQ-9 severity. The training set is roughly balanced (class~0: 5\,054; class~1: 4\,494). Sigmoid recalibration \cite{platt1999calibration} via 5-fold stratified CV maps raw decision scores $s(x)$ to posterior probabilities:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{cal}}(y{=}1 \mid x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{A \cdot s(x) + B}}
\label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Quantile Regressors.} Three gradient boosting regressors (150 rounds each) trained with pinball loss \cite{meinshausen2006quantile} at quantile levels $q \in \{0.10, 0.50, 0.90\}$ produce a distributional prediction envelope for the PHQ-9 total:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_q = \arg\min_y \sum_i \rho_q(y_i - y),\; \rho_q(u) = u\,(q - \mathbf{1}_{u<0})
\label{eq:quantile}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Explanation Proxy.} A 100-tree Random Forest (depth 5) is trained separately to enable SHAP's TreeExplainer \cite{lundberg2017shap}, which computes exact Shapley values:

\begin{equation}
\phi_j = \!\!\sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!(|F|{-}|S|{-}1)!}{|F|!}\big[f(S {\cup} \{j\}) - f(S)\big]
\label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}

The dashboard surfaces the three highest-magnitude attributions as natural-language insight statements.

\subsection{Classifier Performance}

On an 80/20 stratified holdout, the calibrated classifier yields AUROC~$\approx$~0.567 and a Brier score of 0.245. Fig.~\ref{fig:roc} plots the operating characteristic. These figures sit below the thresholds that would support standalone predictive use, and we present them without embellishment. The performance ceiling traces directly to how the training corpus was assembled: two structurally distinct survey instruments---one measuring sleep and lifestyle, the other measuring depressive symptoms---were joined through demographic matching, a procedure that preserves marginal distributions but cannot reconstruct the conditional dependencies that would exist in a natively integrated dataset. Inflating these numbers by injecting synthetic correlations was a path we explicitly rejected; the resulting AUROC would be higher but epistemically hollow.

Fig.~\ref{fig:feat} ranks the ten input features by impurity-based importance from the SHAP proxy forest. Age dominates, followed by Stress Level and Cycle Day. The three temporal rolling features---Stress Volatility, Stress Rolling Mean, and Sleep Rolling Mean---collectively account for a non-negligible share, which suggests that even rudimentary longitudinal encoding captures information absent from single-day snapshots. \subsection{Formula Sensitivity}

To verify that the deterministic formula behaves as intended, we swept each input variable across its full range while holding the others at baseline. Fig.~\ref{fig:sens} shows the stress sweep. Risk climbs steadily from~15 at stress~1 to~55 at stress~8, crossing into the Moderate band around stress~5.5. Reaching the High band ($R \geq 65$) requires simultaneous adversity on multiple axes---high stress coupled with poor sleep or elevated anxiety---which is a deliberate design property: no single factor, however extreme, should by itself trigger the highest severity tier. The transition points correspond well with clinical characterizations of the stress--health relationship \cite{mcewen2008central}.

\subsection{Hormonal Phase Effects}

Fig.~\ref{fig:cycle} isolates the effect of cycle phase on baseline risk under otherwise identical conditions. The Luteal window (days 17--28, $\phi = +8$) pushes risk to approximately 36---just into the Moderate tier---whereas the Ovulatory window (days 14--16, $\phi = -8$) holds it near 20. A 16-point spread may appear small against the 0--100 scale, but this restraint is intentional: hormonal context should modulate, not dominate, the composite assessment. Population-level effect sizes in the cycle-mood literature are themselves moderate \cite{baker2007sleep, kiesner2012menstrual}, and overstating them computationally would misrepresent the evidence.

For users who select Male or Other, $\phi$ defaults to zero. The 10\% capacity that would have gone to cycle context shifts to sleep deficit (25\%~$\to$~30\%), preserving total weight parity (Table~\ref{tab:weights}).

\subsection{The Case for a Hybrid Design}

A fair objection is that the ML components, given their limited standalone discrimination, could simply be omitted. We retain them for four distinct reasons. First, the deterministic formula handles the primary user-facing score, supplying full auditability. Second, the calibrated classifier provides a complementary probability estimate shaped by the full PHQ-9 feature space, capturing interaction effects that a linear sum misses. Third, the quantile regressors produce confidence intervals---something no fixed formula can generate---allowing the dashboard to communicate uncertainty honestly. Fourth, the SHAP proxy translates model logic into plain-language attributions, enabling the ``Personalized Insights'' cards that distinguish the dashboard from a bare numerical readout.

\subsection{Limitations}

We close this section with an honest inventory of constraints. The evaluation cohort is synthetic; although its feature distributions were designed to span clinically realistic ranges, it cannot reproduce the complex interdependencies found in organic behavioral data. Formula weights reflect expert judgment rather than optimization against clinical endpoints. The classifier's AUROC is a direct consequence of cross-dataset matching and would almost certainly improve with a natively integrated training corpus. Cycle-phase modifiers represent population averages and do not yet personalize to individual hormonal profiles. Most critically, every output of this system is a Behavioral Wellness Estimate---never a clinical diagnosis---and should be understood as such.

%-----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

This paper presented LunaAura, an explainable framework that bridges deterministic composite scoring, calibrated probabilistic classification, quantile uncertainty estimation, and instance-level SHAP attribution within a longitudinal behavioral health dashboard. The system demonstrates that cycle-aware, gender-adaptive wellness analytics can be built from established ML components and transparent formulas without resorting to opaque end-to-end models or inflated performance claims.

Planned extensions fall into three categories. Clinically, the highest priority is evaluation against longitudinal datasets collected under informed consent, with verified psychiatric outcomes as ground truth. Technically, integration with wearable APIs---heart rate variability, actigraphy, sleep staging---would shift the input mix from purely self-reported to a blend of active and passive signals \cite{torous2021digital}. Architecturally, federated learning \cite{wu2021federated} would allow model refinement across geographically distributed cohorts without centralizing raw behavioral records, a property essential for privacy-sensitive deployment. Smaller-scale improvements include Bayesian search over formula weights conditioned on clinical outcomes, native mobile deployment, and individualized cycle-length modeling to replace the current fixed 28-day assumption.

All source code, trained model artifacts, database schemas, and evaluation scripts reside in the project repository for independent replication and extension.
