\begin{abstract}
Most digital wellness applications function as generic trackers, producing one-size-fits-all feedback that ignores individual behavioral rhythms and physiological context. This paper presents LunaAura, a full-stack explainable machine learning framework that brings together deterministic composite risk scoring, calibrated gradient-boosted classification, quantile regression for uncertainty-aware PHQ-9 estimation, and SHAP-driven explanations within a single longitudinal monitoring platform. The system tracks six daily behavioral signals---sleep duration, perceived stress, physical activity, anxiety, hydration, and menstrual cycle day---and fuses them into personalized wellness estimates through a transparent weighted formula. Female users benefit from additive menstrual-phase modifiers drawn from chronobiological evidence; male and non-binary users receive re-normalized weights that preserve analytical depth. A Flask REST backend, SQLite persistence layer, and Chart.js interactive dashboard complete the architecture. Evaluation on a controlled synthetic cohort of 105 users and 470 daily log entries, together with a 40-day longitudinal case study, confirms the formula's sensitivity to clinically meaningful input variations. The platform is framed throughout as a decision-support research prototype, not a clinical diagnostic tool.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
explainable AI, behavioral wellness, hormonal health, personalized analytics, menstrual cycle modeling, risk stratification, quantile regression
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

According to the World Health Organization, mental health conditions now affect approximately one in every eight individuals worldwide, making them a leading cause of disability and lost productivity \cite{who2022mental}. Among university students and early-career professionals, academic workloads, financial pressures, social isolation, and chronic sleep disruption compound in ways that often go unrecognized until a crisis point is reached \cite{adler2022burnout}. Standardized screening tools such as the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) \cite{kroenke2001phq9} remain the clinical gold standard for assessing depressive symptom severity, yet they were conceived for periodic in-clinic encounters---not the kind of continuous, day-to-day monitoring that could catch early warning signs before they escalate.

The proliferation of smartphones has sparked considerable interest in digital phenotyping, the idea that passively collected behavioral signals can serve as proxies for subjective mental states \cite{saeb2015mobile}. Call logs, mobility traces, and screen-time patterns have all been shown to correlate with PHQ-9 outcomes \cite{torous2019digital}. Despite these promising findings, most consumer wellness applications have yet to capitalize on them. The typical wellness app amounts to a glorified diary: users log a mood emoji, receive a generic tip, and disengage within weeks because the feedback feels impersonal and shallow.

Three particular shortcomings stand out. First, nearly every commercial tool applies uniform thresholds---``you slept fewer than seven hours''---without recognizing that six hours of sleep paired with low stress and vigorous exercise is a wholly different scenario from six hours paired with chronic anxiety and inactivity. Multi-dimensional risk assessment, where several factors interact to shape overall wellbeing, remains rare outside research prototypes. Second, the menstrual cycle, a biological rhythm affecting roughly half the global population, is almost entirely absent from wellness scoring models. Yet decades of clinical research confirm that hormonal fluctuations across the roughly 28-day cycle alter sleep architecture \cite{baker2007sleep}, modulate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis \cite{mcewen2008central}, and shift subjective mood in ways that can reverse direction between individuals \cite{kiesner2012menstrual}. Any wellness system that ignores these dynamics risks producing systematically misleading scores for female users. Third, even when machine learning is deployed, the resulting models tend to operate as black boxes. Users receive a number without understanding which inputs drove it or what they might do differently. Trust erodes, engagement drops, and the system fails to deliver on its promise.

LunaAura was built to address each of these gaps. It is not a clinical diagnostic tool---that would require prospective validation with ground-truth psychiatric outcomes---but rather an explainable decision-support framework that makes the connections between daily habits and overall wellbeing visible and actionable. The principal contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item A transparent, six-factor composite risk formula with auditable weights and menstrual-cycle-phase modifiers grounded in published chronobiology.
\item A gender-adaptive scoring architecture that re-normalizes factor contributions for male and non-binary users rather than simply hiding features.
\item A hybrid inference pipeline integrating calibrated HistGradientBoosting classification, three-quantile distributional regression, and SHAP-based per-instance explanations.
\item A complete, reproducible implementation encompassing data pipeline, model training, REST API, SQLite persistence, and an interactive longitudinal dashboard.
\end{itemize}

\section{Literature Review}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Machine Learning in Mental Health}
Computational approaches to mental health screening have grown rapidly over the past decade. The PHQ-9, originally validated by Kroenke, Spitzer, and Williams \cite{kroenke2001phq9}, has become a benchmark target for automated severity estimation. Arefin and de Roux \cite{arefin2020machine} showed that ensemble classifiers can distinguish depressive from non-depressive language patterns in social media posts, achieving reasonable accuracy on cross-sectional data. However, social-media-based approaches raise privacy concerns and typically lack the longitudinal dimension needed to track within-person change over time. Random Forest ensembles \cite{breiman2001random} remain popular for tabular health data due to their robustness to missing values and natural support for feature importance extraction.

\subsection{Digital Phenotyping and Passive Sensing}
Saeb et al.\ \cite{saeb2015mobile} demonstrated that GPS-derived mobility features and phone usage statistics from smartphones correlate with self-reported PHQ-9 scores, establishing the feasibility of passive behavioral monitoring without requiring active user input. The MONARCA system \cite{bardram2020designing} extended this paradigm to bipolar disorder management, combining passively sensed data with self-reported mood in a longitudinal framework. More recently, Torous and colleagues \cite{torous2019digital, torous2021digital} have advocated for rigorous clinical validation of digital phenotyping pipelines, emphasizing that data quality, informed consent, and model interpretability remain unresolved barriers.

\subsection{Explainable Artificial Intelligence}
The SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) framework introduced by Lundberg and Lee \cite{lundberg2017shap} provides a unified, theoretically grounded method for computing per-feature contributions to individual model predictions. In health applications, explainability is not merely a nicety but a practical requirement: clinicians need to understand why a model flagged a patient, and end users need actionable insights rather than opaque scores. Research on EmotionSense \cite{rachuri2010emotionsense} further demonstrated that user engagement with wellness tools improves measurably when feedback is contextual and interpretable rather than generic.

\subsection{Cycle-Aware Health Systems}
Baker and Driver \cite{baker2007sleep} documented that slow-wave sleep activity drops significantly during the luteal phase of the menstrual cycle, a finding with direct implications for next-day cognitive performance and mood. Kiesner \cite{kiesner2012menstrual} observed that the same luteal-phase hormonal shifts produce paradoxically opposite affective outcomes across different women, a result that argues strongly for personalized baselines rather than population-level averages. McEwen \cite{mcewen2008central} provided a neuroendocrine framework explaining how chronic stress hormone exposure amplifies the biological vulnerability windows created by cyclical hormonal fluctuations. Despite this well-established literature, computational wellness platforms have been slow to incorporate cycle awareness into their scoring models.

\subsection{Probability Calibration and Uncertainty Quantification}
Platt scaling \cite{platt1999calibration} fits a logistic sigmoid to a classifier's raw output scores, transforming them into well-calibrated probability estimates---an important step when downstream decisions depend on predicted probabilities rather than hard class labels. Quantile regression \cite{koenker2001quantile, meinshausen2006quantile} offers a complementary approach: by estimating conditional quantiles rather than the conditional mean, it produces distributional uncertainty bounds without imposing parametric assumptions. For health analytics, where over-confident predictions carry real costs, these techniques are especially valuable.

\section{Methodology}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{System Architecture}
LunaAura follows a three-tier design. The frontend, built with HTML, vanilla JavaScript, and Chart.js for visualization, communicates with a Flask REST backend running on port 5001. The backend orchestrates four subsystems: a hybrid prediction engine combining deterministic risk scoring with ML inference, a SHAP-based TreeExplainer module, a rules-based recommendation generator, and a chart simulation module that produces visualization-ready data arrays. All user data persists in a SQLite database. Seven REST endpoints serve the application: \texttt{/health} (system status), \texttt{/signup} and \texttt{/login} (authentication), \texttt{/profile} (demographic updates), \texttt{/predict} (inference with automatic logging), \texttt{/analytics} (cohort-level aggregates), and \texttt{/insights} (model metadata).

\subsection{Database Schema}
The \texttt{users} table stores 12 fields: username, password hash, age, gender, height, weight, cycle length, sleep target, cohort group, source tag, and creation/update timestamps. The \texttt{user\_history} table stores 12 fields per daily entry: sleep duration, stress level (1--10 ordinal), mood score, cycle day, menstrual phase label, physical activity (minutes), computed wellness score, predicted risk percentage, anxiety level (1--10), and water intake (litres), linked to \texttt{users} via foreign key.

\subsection{Synthetic Cohort}
Clinical data collection was outside the scope of this prototype, so a controlled synthetic cohort was generated with a fixed random seed (42) to ensure full reproducibility. The cohort comprises 105 users distributed across three risk strata: 30 high-risk profiles (sleep 3--5.5\,h, stress 7--10, activity 0--20\,min), 40 moderate-risk (sleep 5.5--7\,h, stress 4--7, activity 20--60\,min), and 30 low-risk (sleep 7--9\,h, stress 1--4, activity 60--120\,min). Gender distribution is 86 female, 14 male, and 5 other. Ages range from 18 to 55 years (mean 36.5). A total of 470 daily log entries populate the history table. One user (Eshita) was generated with 85 sequential entries spanning 40+ days, incorporating realistic cycle-phase-driven behavioral variation: hormone-linked sleep reduction during luteal and menstrual phases, compensatory stress elevation, and reduced activity during symptomatic windows.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
The ML training pipeline operates on a separate master dataset ($n = 9{,}548$ records, 28 columns) that merges sleep-lifestyle survey data with PHQ-9 depression screening responses via K-nearest-neighbor linking on gender and age, followed by 14-day sequential expansion to simulate longitudinal tracking windows. Ten numeric features are retained for model input: Age, Sleep Duration, Physical Activity Level, Stress Level, Base Cycle Length, Cycle Day, Hormone Proxy (a sinusoidal encoding of cycle position), Sleep Rolling Mean (3-day window), Stress Rolling Mean (3-day window), and Stress Volatility (3-day standard deviation). The rolling features capture short-term behavioral momentum that isolated daily snapshots would miss.

\subsection{Composite Risk Formula}
The primary risk scoring mechanism is a deterministic weighted composite, deliberately chosen over pure ML prediction for three reasons: full transparency (every weight is auditable), perfect reproducibility (identical inputs always yield identical outputs), and immediate interpretability (users can trace any score change to a specific input). The composite risk $R$ is computed as:

\begin{equation}
R = \sum_{i=1}^{5} f_i(x_i) \;+\; \tau \;+\; \phi(d, g)
\label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}

\noindent where each factor contribution is:

\begin{align}
f_1 &= \frac{x_{\text{stress}}}{10} \times 35 \label{eq:f1}\\[3pt]
f_2 &= \max\!\Bigl(0,\;\frac{8 - x_{\text{sleep}}}{8}\Bigr) \times 25 \label{eq:f2}\\[3pt]
f_3 &= \frac{x_{\text{anxiety}}}{10} \times 20 \label{eq:f3}\\[3pt]
f_4 &= \max\!\Bigl(0,\;\frac{60 - x_{\text{activity}}}{60}\Bigr) \times 10 \label{eq:f4}\\[3pt]
f_5 &= \max\!\Bigl(0,\;\frac{2.5 - x_{\text{water}}}{2.5}\Bigr) \times 5 \label{eq:f5}
\end{align}

\noindent The constant $\tau = 5$ represents a fixed trend baseline penalty. The cycle-phase modifier $\phi$ is defined for female users as:

\begin{equation}
\phi(d, g) = \begin{cases}
+5 & d \in [1,5] \;\text{(Menstrual)}\\
-5 & d \in [6,13] \;\text{(Follicular)}\\
-8 & d \in [14,16] \;\text{(Ovulatory)}\\
+8 & d \in [17,28] \;\text{(Luteal)}\\
0  & g \neq \text{Female}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:phase}
\end{equation}

\noindent Risk is clamped to $[0,100]$ and classified as Low ($R<35$), Moderate ($35 \leq R < 65$), or High ($R \geq 65$).

\subsection{Wellness Score}

A complementary wellness score aggregates normalized factor dimensions $\hat{x}_j \in [0,100]$:

\begin{equation}
W = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \alpha_j \cdot \hat{x}_j, \quad W \in [0,100]
\label{eq:wellness}
\end{equation}

\noindent For female users ($k=8$): Sleep 25\%, Stress 20\%, Activity 15\%, Anxiety 15\%, Cycle Context 10\%, Water 5\%, Age 5\%, Stability 5\%. For male/other users ($k=6$), cycle context is removed and weights are re-normalized: Sleep 30\%, Stress 25\%, Activity 20\%, Anxiety 15\%, Water 5\%, Age 5\%. Individual normalization functions include $\hat{x}_{\text{sleep}} = 100 - |x_{\text{sleep}} - 8| \times 15$ (penalizing deviation from the 8-hour optimum), $\hat{x}_{\text{stress}} = 100 - (x_{\text{stress}} - 1) \times 11.1$ (inverse linear mapping), and $\hat{x}_{\text{activity}} = \min(100,\; x_{\text{activity}} / 60 \times 100)$ (scaled against a 60-minute target).

\subsection{Machine Learning Pipeline}

Three ML models supplement the deterministic formula to provide capabilities that a fixed formula cannot offer on its own:

\textbf{Calibrated Classifier.} A \texttt{HistGradientBoostingClassifier} (max\_iter\,=\,100, max\_leaf\_nodes\,=\,31, learning rate\,=\,0.05) is trained on the master dataset to predict PHQ-9-derived referral flags (class 0: $n=5{,}054$; class 1: $n=4{,}494$). Post-hoc sigmoid calibration via \texttt{CalibratedClassifierCV} with 5-fold stratified cross-validation \cite{platt1999calibration} transforms raw scores into calibrated probabilities:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{cal}}(y=1 \mid x) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\big(A \cdot s(x) + B\big)}
\label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $s(x)$ is the base model's raw decision function output and parameters $A$, $B$ are fitted via maximum likelihood on held-out folds.

\textbf{Quantile Regressors.} Three \texttt{HistGradientBoostingRegressor} models (max\_iter\,=\,150) target the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles of the PHQ-9 total score distribution using pinball loss \cite{meinshausen2006quantile}:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_q = \arg\min_y \sum_i \rho_q(y_i - y), \quad \rho_q(u) = u\,(q - \mathbf{1}_{u<0})
\label{eq:quantile}
\end{equation}

\noindent The resulting 10th--90th percentile envelope provides distributional uncertainty bounds without imposing parametric assumptions \cite{koenker2001quantile}.

\textbf{SHAP Explainer.} A separate \texttt{RandomForestClassifier} (100 estimators, max depth 5) is trained as a SHAP-compatible proxy. TreeExplainer \cite{lundberg2017shap} computes exact Shapley values for each feature:

\begin{equation}
\phi_j = \!\!\!\sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!\,(|F|-|S|-1)!}{|F|!}\big[f(S \cup \{j\}) - f(S)\big]
\label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}

\noindent The three highest-magnitude features are surfaced to the user as natural-language explanations on the dashboard.

\section{Results and Discussion}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{ML Model Performance}

The calibrated HistGradientBoosting classifier achieves an AUROC of approximately 0.567 on the master dataset's 80/20 train-test split, with a Brier calibration score of 0.245. Fig.~\ref{fig:roc} shows the ROC curve. These numbers are modest by conventional ML benchmarks, and we report them without embellishment. The explanation is straightforward: the training data was constructed by bridging two heterogeneous source datasets---a sleep-lifestyle survey and a depression screening questionnaire---using only shared demographic variables (age, gender). This statistical linkage inevitably introduces substantial noise, because the behavioral patterns of one individual in the sleep dataset have no guaranteed correspondence to the mental health outcomes of the matched individual in the depression dataset. Rather than engineering synthetic correlation variables to inflate the AUROC (a temptation that would compromise scientific integrity), LunaAura acknowledges this constraint honestly and positions the ML classifier as a supplementary component within a hybrid architecture.

The high-risk threshold of 65 is reached only when stress combines with poor sleep or high anxiety---a design feature that prevents any single factor from triggering a high-risk classification in isolation. Fig.~\ref{fig:cycle} compares the baseline risk scores produced by the four menstrual cycle phases under otherwise identical input conditions. The Luteal phase (days 17--28) generates the highest baseline risk at approximately 36---just crossing into the moderate range---while the Ovulatory phase (days 14--16) produces the lowest at approximately 20. \subsection{Why a Hybrid Architecture}

A reasonable question is why LunaAura retains ML components given their modest standalone accuracy. The answer lies in complementary functionality that no single approach can provide alone. The deterministic formula delivers the user-facing risk score with complete transparency---every user can trace exactly how their inputs translate to their score. The calibrated classifier contributes a probability estimate informed by the PHQ-9 training distribution, capturing nonlinear feature interactions that a linear weighted sum cannot. The quantile regressors supply uncertainty intervals, honestly communicating how much confidence the system places in any given estimate. And the SHAP explainer bridges the gap between model internals and human understanding, generating per-instance attributions that the dashboard renders as natural-language insight cards.

\subsection{Limitations and Honest Constraints}

Several limitations must be acknowledged candidly. The evaluation cohort is synthetically generated from controlled distributions; while the feature ranges mirror real-world values, the absence of organically arising behavioral correlations constrains generalizability claims. The composite formula's weights are expert-derived heuristics, not the product of data-driven optimization against clinical outcomes. The ML classifier's modest AUROC reflects the fundamental difficulty of bridging heterogeneous datasets, and we consider honest reporting of this reality far preferable to metric inflation. Cycle-phase modifiers are based on population-level findings and do not yet accommodate the substantial between-individual variation documented by Kiesner \cite{kiesner2012menstrual}. Finally, and most importantly, the system produces Behavioral Wellness Estimates---not clinical diagnoses---and must never be interpreted as a replacement for professional mental health evaluation.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
%-----------------------------------------------------------------------

LunaAura demonstrates that a personalized, explainable, cycle-aware behavioral wellness framework is technically achievable using well-established machine learning techniques combined with a transparent deterministic scoring layer. The system's principal value lies not in any single algorithmic novelty but in the careful integration of multiple complementary components---composite risk formulas, calibrated probabilistic classification, quantile uncertainty estimation, SHAP-based explainability, and longitudinal interactive visualization---into a cohesive user experience that respects both scientific rigor and practical usability.

Several directions for future work emerge naturally. First and most critically, validation on clinically collected longitudinal datasets with ground-truth psychiatric outcomes would move the system from research prototype toward potential deployment. Second, integration with wearable device APIs (accelerometers, optical heart-rate sensors, sleep-stage trackers) would supplement self-reported inputs with passively collected physiological signals, reducing reporting burden and improving data fidelity \cite{torous2021digital}. Third, federated learning architectures \cite{wu2021federated} could enable model improvement across distributed user populations without centralizing sensitive behavioral data, addressing a core privacy concern. Additional planned enhancements include Bayesian optimization of formula weights against clinical outcome data, mobile-native deployment for continuous monitoring, and expansion of the cycle-aware framework to accommodate user-specific cycle irregularity patterns rather than a fixed 28-day assumption.

The complete codebase, trained model artifacts, database schema, and evaluation assets are available in the project repository, enabling independent reproduction and extension of all results reported in this paper.
