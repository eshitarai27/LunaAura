\begin{abstract}
Existing digital wellness applications largely function as static trackers, offering generalized feedback without accommodating individual behavioral rhythms or physiological context such as menstrual cycle dynamics. This paper presents LunaAura, a full-stack explainable machine learning framework for hormonal and emotional health analysis. The system integrates a deterministic composite risk scoring formula, a calibrated HistGradientBoosting classifier with sigmoid post-hoc calibration, quantile-based distributional regression for uncertainty-aware PHQ-9 estimation, and SHAP-driven per-instance explanations into a unified longitudinal monitoring platform. Six daily behavioral signals---sleep duration, perceived stress, physical activity, anxiety, hydration, and menstrual cycle day---are synthesized into personalized wellness estimates and risk indicators through a transparent weighted composite formula. For female users, additive menstrual phase modifiers grounded in chronobiological literature adjust baseline risk; for male and non-binary users, factor weights are re-normalized to preserve equitable analytical depth. The architecture comprises a Flask REST backend, SQLite persistence layer, and Chart.js-powered interactive dashboard rendering longitudinal trend projections, factor breakdowns, and natural-language explanations. Evaluation on a controlled synthetic cohort of 105 users across 470 daily log entries, supplemented by a 40-day longitudinal case study, demonstrates the formula's sensitivity to clinically meaningful input variations. The platform is positioned as a decision-support research prototype with transparent methodology and honest disclosure of limitations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ======================================================================

Mental health conditions affect roughly one in eight people globally, yet the gap between those who need support and those who receive it remains wide~\cite{who2022mental}. University students and young professionals face compounding pressures---academic workloads, social isolation, financial stress, and disrupted sleep routines---that frequently go unmonitored until they become clinically significant~\cite{adler2022burnout}. Standardized screening instruments like the PHQ-9~\cite{kroenke2001phq9} provide validated snapshot assessments, but they were designed for periodic clinical encounters rather than continuous self-monitoring.

Smartphones and wearable devices have opened new avenues for longitudinal behavioral tracking. Research in digital phenotyping has established that passively collected signals---call patterns, mobility traces, sleep-wake rhythms---correlate meaningfully with depressive symptom severity~\cite{saeb2015mobile, torous2019digital}. Yet most consumer wellness applications reduce this potential to simplistic step counters or mood diaries, offering little analytical depth and no personalization beyond surface-level customization.

Three specific gaps motivate this work. First, most tools treat users as interchangeable, applying identical thresholds regardless of individual baselines. Second, few systems account for menstrual cycle dynamics despite substantial evidence that hormonal fluctuations modulate sleep architecture, stress reactivity, and mood regulation across the approximately 28-day cycle~\cite{baker2007sleep, kiesner2012menstrual}. Roughly half the global population experiences these cyclical variations, yet they remain invisible to most platforms. Third, interpretability remains an afterthought---users receive scores without understanding which factors drive them.

LunaAura addresses these gaps through a personalized, explainable, cycle-aware behavioral analytics framework. Rather than attempting clinical diagnosis, the system provides Behavioral Wellness Estimates and Risk Indicators that help users understand the interplay among their daily habits. The principal contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
\item A transparent six-factor composite risk formula with auditable weights and cycle-phase modifiers grounded in published chronobiological findings.
\item A gender-adaptive scoring architecture that re-normalizes factor contributions for male and non-binary users without reducing analytical depth.
\item Integration of calibrated gradient boosting classification, quantile regression for uncertainty estimation, and SHAP-based per-instance explainability within a single inference pipeline.
\item A complete open-source implementation spanning data pipeline, ML training, REST API, and interactive longitudinal dashboard.
\end{itemize}

\section{Research Problem}
% ======================================================================

Despite advances in machine learning for health applications, behavioral wellness tools exhibit persistent shortcomings.

\textbf{Static Thresholding.} Consumer apps apply fixed cutoffs without considering multi-dimensional interaction. A person sleeping six hours under low stress occupies a fundamentally different risk profile than someone sleeping six hours under high stress and sedentary conditions.

\textbf{Absence of Longitudinal Modeling.} Most tools treat entries independently, discarding temporal context. They cannot detect that stress has trended upward for two weeks or that sleep quality deteriorated following a hormonal phase transition.

\textbf{Neglect of Physiological Context.} Menstrual cycle phases exert documented effects on sleep quality~\cite{baker2007sleep}, stress hormone regulation~\cite{mcewen2008central}, and subjective wellbeing~\cite{kiesner2012menstrual}. The luteal phase (days 17--28) is associated with elevated baseline stress and disrupted sleep. Ignoring this introduces systematic bias for female users while offering no adaptive pathway for male users.

\textbf{Poor Interpretability.} Even ML-powered systems typically produce opaque scores. The inability to understand \textit{why} a score changed undermines trust and prevents informed behavioral adjustment.

LunaAura addresses each limitation within a single integrated platform while remaining transparent about the boundaries of a research prototype evaluated on synthetic data.

\section{Review of Literature}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{Machine Learning for Mental Health Screening}
Kroenke et al.\ established the PHQ-9 as a reliable depression severity measure~\cite{kroenke2001phq9}. Subsequent work explored automated scoring from behavioral proxies; Arefin and de Roux~\cite{arefin2020machine} demonstrated that ensemble classifiers can distinguish depressive language patterns from social media features. However, these approaches typically operate on cross-sectional snapshots rather than longitudinal behavioral streams.

\subsection{Digital Phenotyping and Passive Sensing}
Saeb et al.~\cite{saeb2015mobile} showed that GPS mobility and phone usage statistics correlate with PHQ-9 scores. The MONARCA system~\cite{bardram2020designing} extended passive sensing to bipolar disorder monitoring. Torous et al.~\cite{torous2019digital, torous2021digital} have advocated for clinically validated digital phenotyping pipelines, noting that data quality and interpretability remain significant barriers.

\subsection{Explainable AI in Health Applications}
Lundberg and Lee's SHAP framework~\cite{lundberg2017shap} provides theoretically grounded feature attribution. In health contexts, explainability serves both clinical requirements and user engagement---transparent feedback encourages sustained platform use~\cite{rachuri2010emotionsense}.

\subsection{Cycle-Aware Health Systems}
Baker and Driver~\cite{baker2007sleep} documented significant sleep architecture differences across menstrual phases. Kiesner~\cite{kiesner2012menstrual} found paradoxically opposite mood effects across individuals, underscoring the need for personalized baselines. Despite this evidence, few computational wellness systems incorporate cycle awareness.

\subsection{Calibration and Uncertainty Quantification}
Platt scaling~\cite{platt1999calibration} provides post-hoc probability calibration, while quantile regression~\cite{meinshausen2006quantile, koenker2001quantile} offers distributional uncertainty estimates without parametric assumptions---particularly relevant for health applications where honest uncertainty bounds carry more value than point predictions.

\section{Methodology}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{System Architecture}

LunaAura follows a three-tier architecture: a static frontend (HTML/JavaScript/Chart.js), a Flask REST API backend on port 5001, and a SQLite persistence layer. The backend orchestrates four subsystems: (i)~a hybrid prediction engine combining deterministic risk scoring with ML inference, (ii)~a SHAP-based TreeExplainer, (iii)~a rules-based recommendation generator, and (iv)~a chart simulation module producing visualization-ready data arrays.

Seven REST endpoints serve the application: \texttt{/health} (status), \texttt{/signup} and \texttt{/login} (authentication), \texttt{/profile} (profile updates), \texttt{/predict} (inference + logging), \texttt{/analytics} (cohort aggregates), and \texttt{/insights} (model metadata).

\subsection{Database Schema}

The SQLite database (\texttt{lunaaura.db}) contains two core tables. The \texttt{users} table (12 columns) stores demographic anchors: username, password hash, age, gender, height, weight, cycle length, sleep target, cohort group, and timestamps. The \texttt{user\_history} table (12 columns) captures daily logs: sleep duration, stress level (1--10), mood score, cycle day, menstrual phase, physical activity (minutes), wellness score, predicted risk, anxiety level (1--10), and water intake (litres), linked via foreign key to \texttt{users.id}.

\subsection{Synthetic Cohort Generation}

A controlled synthetic cohort was generated using a deterministic seeding procedure (seed~=~42). The cohort comprises 105 users across three risk strata: 30 high-risk (sleep 3--5.5h, stress 7--10, activity 0--20~min), 40 moderate-risk (sleep 5.5--7h, stress 4--7, activity 20--60~min), and 30 low-risk (sleep 7--9h, stress 1--4, activity 60--120~min). Gender follows an 80/15/5 split (Female/Male/Other), ages span 18--55, and 470 daily log entries populate the history table. One user (Eshita) was generated with 40 sequential days incorporating cycle-phase-driven behavioral variation.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

The ML training pipeline operates on a separate master dataset ($n = 9{,}548$ records, 28 columns) merging sleep-lifestyle data with PHQ-9 depression screening responses via K-NN linking on gender and age with 14-day sequential expansion. Ten numeric features are retained:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Age, Sleep Duration, Physical Activity Level, Stress Level
\item Base Cycle Length, Cycle Day
\item Hormone Proxy (sinusoidal cycle encoding)
\item Sleep Rolling Mean (3-day), Stress Rolling Mean (3-day), Stress Volatility (3-day $\sigma$)
\end{enumerate}

The rolling-window features capture short-term behavioral momentum rather than isolated daily snapshots.

\subsection{Composite Risk Formula}

The primary risk scoring mechanism is a deterministic weighted composite, chosen for its transparency, auditability, and reproducibility. Given behavioral inputs, the composite risk score $R$ is computed as:

\begin{equation}
R = \sum_{i=1}^{5} w_i \cdot f_i(x_i) + \tau + \phi(d, g)
\label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}

\noindent where each factor contribution $f_i$ is defined as:

\begin{align}
f_1 &= \frac{x_{\text{stress}}}{10} \times 35 & \text{(stress, } w_1{=}35\%) \label{eq:f1}\\
f_2 &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{8 - x_{\text{sleep}}}{8}\right) \times 25 & \text{(sleep deficit)} \label{eq:f2}\\
f_3 &= \frac{x_{\text{anxiety}}}{10} \times 20 & \text{(anxiety, } w_3{=}20\%) \label{eq:f3}\\
f_4 &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{60 - x_{\text{activity}}}{60}\right) \times 10 & \text{(activity deficit)} \label{eq:f4}\\
f_5 &= \max\!\left(0,\;\frac{2.5 - x_{\text{water}}}{2.5}\right) \times 5 & \text{(hydration deficit)} \label{eq:f5}
\end{align}

The term $\tau = 5$ is a fixed trend baseline. The cycle-phase modifier $\phi(d, g)$ is defined for female users ($g = \text{Female}$) as:

\begin{equation}
\phi(d, g) = \begin{cases}
+5 & d \in [1, 5] \;\text{(Menstrual)}\\
-5 & d \in [6, 13] \;\text{(Follicular)}\\
-8 & d \in [14, 16] \;\text{(Ovulatory)}\\
+8 & d \in [17, 28] \;\text{(Luteal)}\\
0  & g \neq \text{Female}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:phase}
\end{equation}

Risk is clamped to $[0, 100]$ and classified as Low ($R < 35$), Moderate ($35 \leq R < 65$), or High ($R \geq 65$).

\subsection{Wellness Score Computation}

A complementary wellness score $W$ aggregates normalized factor dimensions $\hat{x}_i \in [0, 100]$:

\begin{equation}
W = \sum_{j=1}^{k} \alpha_j \cdot \hat{x}_j, \quad W \in [0, 100]
\label{eq:wellness}
\end{equation}

For female users ($k{=}8$): Sleep 25\%, Stress 20\%, Activity 15\%, Anxiety 15\%, Cycle Context 10\%, Water 5\%, Age 5\%, Stability 5\%. For male/other users ($k{=}6$), cycle context is removed and weights re-normalized: Sleep 30\%, Stress 25\%, Activity 20\%, Anxiety 15\%, Water 5\%, Age 5\%.

Normalization functions include: $\hat{x}_{\text{sleep}} = 100 - |x_{\text{sleep}} - 8| \times 15$ (penalizing deviation from 8h optimum), $\hat{x}_{\text{stress}} = 100 - (x_{\text{stress}} - 1) \times 11.1$ (inverse linear), and $\hat{x}_{\text{activity}} = (x_{\text{activity}} / 60) \times 100$ (scaled to 60-min target).

\subsection{Machine Learning Pipeline}

Three ML models supplement the deterministic formula:

\textbf{Calibrated Classifier.} A \texttt{HistGradientBoostingClassifier} ($\text{max\_iter}{=}100$, $\text{max\_leaf\_nodes}{=}31$, $\eta{=}0.05$) trained on the master dataset to predict PHQ-9-derived referral flags ($n_0{=}5{,}054$, $n_1{=}4{,}494$). Post-hoc probability calibration uses sigmoid-fitted \texttt{CalibratedClassifierCV} with 5-fold stratified cross-validation~\cite{platt1999calibration}:

\begin{equation}
P_{\text{cal}}(y{=}1 \mid x) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(A \cdot s(x) + B)}
\label{eq:platt}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $s(x)$ is the raw classifier score and $A$, $B$ are fitted via maximum likelihood on held-out folds.

\textbf{Quantile Regressors.} Three \texttt{HistGradientBoostingRegressor} models ($\text{max\_iter}{=}150$) target quantiles $q \in \{0.10, 0.50, 0.90\}$ of the PHQ-9 total score, producing an uncertainty-aware prediction interval:

\begin{equation}
\hat{y}_{q}(x) = \arg\min_{y} \sum_{i} \rho_q(y_i - y), \quad \rho_q(u) = u \cdot (q - \mathbf{1}_{u<0})
\label{eq:quantile}
\end{equation}

The 10th--90th percentile envelope provides honest confidence bounds without assuming distributional form~\cite{meinshausen2006quantile}.

\textbf{SHAP Explainer.} A separate \texttt{RandomForestClassifier} ($n_{\text{est}}{=}100$, $\text{max\_depth}{=}5$) serves as a SHAP-compatible proxy. TreeExplainer~\cite{lundberg2017shap} computes Shapley values $\phi_j$ for each feature $j$:

\begin{equation}
\phi_j = \sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!\;(|F|-|S|-1)!}{|F|!} \left[ f(S \cup \{j\}) - f(S) \right]
\label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}

The top three contributing factors are surfaced as natural-language explanations on the dashboard.

\subsection{Recommendation Engine}

A rules-based engine evaluates both ML outputs and raw inputs against domain-informed thresholds: sleep below 6h triggers sleep intervention, stress above 7 triggers stress management, hydration below 4 cups flags nutrition guidance. Recommendations are categorized by priority (Low/Medium/High) and type (maintenance, lifestyle, behavioral alert, nutrition).

\subsection{Frontend Dashboard}

The interactive dashboard renders: (i)~a wellness score ring with animated progress, (ii)~a 7-day risk momentum heatmap, (iii)~30-day trend projections for wellness, stress, and sleep, (iv)~a risk distribution doughnut chart, (v)~a factor contribution breakdown with signed impact values, (vi)~a menstrual phase influence panel (female only), and (vii)~personalized insight cards. For male users, the phase panel is hidden while all other modules render with gender-appropriate weights.

\subsection{Model Selection Rationale}

The hybrid architecture combines deterministic scoring with ML inference. The composite formula was chosen as the primary mechanism for three reasons: full transparency, reproducibility, and interpretability. The ML models serve supplementary roles: calibrated probability estimation, distributional uncertainty bounds, and SHAP-based factor attribution. The HistGradientBoosting family was selected for native missing-value handling and quantile regression compatibility.

\subsection{Feature Importance}

SHAP analysis of the Random Forest proxy (Fig.~\ref{fig:model}d) reveals that Age, Stress Level, and Cycle Day contribute the strongest signals, consistent with domain expectations. Sleep Duration and Physical Activity occupy mid-range importance, while engineered rolling-window features contribute lower but non-trivial importance, suggesting temporal patterns carry predictive value beyond instantaneous measurements.

\section{Results and Discussion}
% ======================================================================

\subsection{ML Model Evaluation}

Fig.~\ref{fig:model} presents the model evaluation panel. The calibrated HistGradientBoosting classifier achieves an AUROC of approximately 0.567 on the master dataset's train-test split, with a Brier calibration score of 0.245. This modest discriminative performance is expected and honestly reported: the training data bridges heterogeneous datasets (sleep-lifestyle surveys and PHQ-9 screening responses) using generic demographic variables, creating an inherently noisy statistical linkage. Rather than engineering artificial correlations to inflate metrics, LunaAura acknowledges this constraint and relies on the deterministic composite formula for primary risk scoring, using the classifier for supplementary distributional estimation.

The quantile regressors provide uncertainty bounds on PHQ-9 point estimates. For a typical healthy input (sleep 8h, stress 2, activity 45~min), the 10th--90th percentile interval spans approximately 3--12 on the 0--27 PHQ-9 scale, reflecting genuine predictive uncertainty rather than false precision.

\subsection{Hybrid Architecture Rationale}

A natural question is why the system retains ML components given their modest standalone performance. The answer lies in complementary functionality. The deterministic formula provides the user-facing risk score with full transparency. The calibrated classifier contributes a probability estimate informed by the broader PHQ-9 training distribution. The quantile regressors supply uncertainty intervals that no deterministic formula can provide. And the SHAP explainer generates per-instance attributions connecting model internals to human-readable explanations. Each component addresses a different facet of the user experience.

\subsection{Composite Formula Validation}

Sensitivity analysis confirms monotonic, clinically plausible responses. Fig.~\ref{fig:sensitivity} shows that composite risk rises from approximately 15 at stress level 1 to 55 at stress level 8, crossing the moderate threshold (35) near stress~$\approx$~5.5. The sleep sensitivity curve demonstrates risk escalation as sleep drops below 6 hours, with the steepest gradient in the 3--5 hour range---consistent with established sleep health thresholds~\cite{hirshkowitz2015sleep}.

The cycle-phase comparison (Fig.~\ref{fig:cycle}) reveals a 16-point baseline risk spread between the most protective phase (Ovulatory, $\phi{=}{-}8$) and the highest-risk phase (Luteal, $\phi{=}{+}8$). While modest relative to the 0--100 scale, this is consistent with reported effect sizes in menstrual cycle research~\cite{baker2007sleep, kiesner2012menstrual} and functions as a contextual modifier rather than a diagnostic signal.

\subsection{Explainability Value}

The SHAP-driven natural-language explanations surfaced on the dashboard (e.g., ``Your current prediction is heavily influenced by reduced sleep duration and elevated stress levels'') serve a dual purpose. For the user, they transform abstract scores into actionable understanding. For academic review, they demonstrate that the system's outputs are traceable to specific input features rather than opaque model internals.

\subsection{Dashboard Effectiveness}

After daily behavioral input submission, all dashboard modules update within two seconds: the wellness ring animates, trend charts extend, factor breakdowns recalculate, and recommendations regenerate. This real-time feedback loop reinforces self-monitoring behavior and makes abstract risk scores tangible through visual context.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations warrant explicit discussion. The evaluation cohort is synthetically generated; while distributions span realistic ranges, the absence of organic behavioral correlations limits generalizability. The composite formula employs expert-derived weights not optimized through clinical outcome data. The classifier's modest AUROC reflects the inherent difficulty of bridging heterogeneous data sources, and we consider honest reporting preferable to metric inflation. Cycle-phase modifiers are population-level estimates and do not account for individual hormonal sensitivity. The system produces Behavioral Wellness Estimates---not clinical diagnoses---and must not replace professional mental health evaluation.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
% ======================================================================

LunaAura demonstrates that a personalized, explainable, cycle-aware behavioral wellness framework is technically feasible using established ML techniques combined with transparent deterministic scoring. The system's strength lies not in novel algorithmic contributions but in its integrative architecture: combining composite risk formulas, calibrated ML inference, quantile uncertainty estimation, SHAP explainability, and longitudinal visualization into a cohesive user experience.

Moving toward deployment readiness requires: (i)~validation on clinically collected longitudinal data with ground-truth mental health outcomes, (ii)~integration with wearable sensor APIs for passively collected physiological signals~\cite{torous2021digital}, and (iii)~investigation of federated learning architectures~\cite{wu2021federated} to enable distributed model improvement without centralizing sensitive data. Additional planned work includes Bayesian weight optimization on clinical outcome data, mobile-native deployment, and expansion of cycle-aware modeling to accommodate individual cycle irregularity patterns.

The codebase, database schema, trained models, and evaluation assets are openly available in the project repository, enabling independent reproduction of all reported results.
